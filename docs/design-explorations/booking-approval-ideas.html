Concept 1 · Decision Card

Lens: Queue & Flow

A single-card-at-a-time dispatcher console. The broker faces one pending booking dead-center, oversized, with all the context they need to decide in 3 seconds: lane, rate vs list, margin, carrier health, and a one-click "Listen to clip" of the negotiation moment. Counter at the top reads 3 of 7 pending like an inbox-zero ritual. Keyboard hints (A approve, R reject, → next) make this a power-user surface — the broker burns through a morning queue without ever touching the mouse.

The right rail shows the next 4 in queue as compressed rows so the broker has lookahead — they can see "Phoenix→Denver coming up, that's a regular" and pre-decide. Approved cards animate up and out, rejected slide down into a collapsed "Rejected today" drawer. The whole screen feels like an air-traffic controller clearing aircraft one at a time, not a spreadsheet.

Concept 2 · Pipeline Board

Lens: Queue & Flow

A three-column kanban — Pending, Approved, Rejected — where every booking is a card and the workflow state IS the spatial position. The broker sees the entire morning's load at once: how many pending, how many already cleared, the running total of approved revenue. Cards carry just enough density (lane, rate, margin pill, MC, carrier health dot) to triage without opening anything. Click a card to expand the right-side detail panel; click the column-header chevron to bulk-approve all visible.

This is for the broker who manages by inventory, not by individual decisions — they want to see "9 pending at 8am, 2 pending at 11am" as a flow-rate, and they want approved bookings to stay visible (with a fade) for the rest of the day so they can audit their own pace. The Approved column doubles as a running ledger.

Concept 3 · Inspector

Lens: Card-Detail Review

The Inspector is a Gmail-style two-pane workspace. The left rail is a dense, scrollable queue of pending bookings rendered as compact rows — MC, lane, rate delta, and a CHS pip — sorted newest-first with a thin amber stripe on the row currently selected. The right pane is an immersive detail surface for one booking at a time: header with carrier identity, a rate-economics block contrasting apply_rate vs loadboard_rate, transcript excerpt, and a metadata grid. Approve / Reject live as a sticky action bar pinned to the bottom of the right pane.

The ergonomic premise is focused triage. The broker reads each booking like an important email — full context loaded, transcript scannable, decision made — then j/k advances. The list never collapses; it stays as a peripheral progress bar so the broker always knows "12 left in the stack." This trades raw browsing speed for confidence-per-click: every approval is a deliberate read.

Concept 4 · Brief

Lens: Card-Detail Review

The Brief keeps the queue as the primary surface — a clean, full-width table of pending bookings — and opens each booking as a Linear-style modal slab that slides over the list with a backdrop scrim. The table stays visible behind, so the broker never loses their place; closing the modal is one keystroke (Esc). The modal itself is a vertical "briefing document": carrier identity at the top, load + economics in the middle, transcript scrollable in the lower half, and an action footer locked to the bottom edge. Margin is rendered large and monospace so the financial gut-check is the first visual hit.

The ergonomic premise is browse-first, dive-on-demand. A broker can power through 80% of bookings from the table alone — the green/red margin column tells them everything they need for a clean approval — and only opens the brief for the ambiguous ones. The modal is a confidence layer, not a workflow gate.

Concept 5 · Bulk Sweep

Lens: Bulk & Batch

Bulk Sweep treats the approval queue as a Sigma-style spreadsheet where every row is a peer and the broker's primary action is multi-select. A persistent action ribbon docks to the top of the table the moment one or more rows are checked, surfacing aggregate context — total committed dollars, total margin, weighted CHS, count of low-quality outliers — so the broker is approving against numbers, not vibes. The metaphor is Gmail's bulk-archive plus a finance-grade summation strip: select rows, see the rollup, sweep them through.

The table is unapologetically dense — Geist Mono numerics, single-pixel borders, sortable headers, fixed first column for the checkbox. A header tri-state checkbox toggles the whole page; a "Select all 47 pending" pill expands beyond the visible viewport. Risk signals (CHS < 70, margin < 5%, missing transcript) render as inline pill warnings. The floor of the screen is a quiet status bar showing the last bulk action ("Approved 12 · 2s ago · undo") so the broker can sweep aggressively without fearing irreversibility.

Concept 6 · Smart Lanes

Lens: Bulk & Batch

Smart Lanes auto-clusters incoming bookings into pre-segmented batches the broker can approve as a unit, reducing N decisions to log(N). The screen is composed of horizontal "lane cards" — each card a cohort grouped by a meaningful axis (lane, carrier, equipment, or risk band) with its own miniature aggregate header and a single dominant "Approve all 6" button. Cards are auto-sorted so the highest-trust cluster sits at the top and one-click-clears in seconds; the lowest-trust cluster collapses to the bottom and forces the broker to expand it before approving anything inside.

Inside each card, individual rows stay visible in a compressed Sigma-style strip with per-row checkboxes — so the broker can de-select a single anomalous booking from an otherwise-clean lane without abandoning the bulk action. Risk bands are color-coded on the left edge (green ≥85, amber 70-84, red <70) giving a peripheral-vision read on the entire queue. The premise: the broker's mental model is "sweep all my repeat Sundance bookings on the DAL→ATL lane" not "row 1, row 2, row 3."

Concept 7 · Clipboard Stack

Lens: Physical / Tactile

The screen is a literal aluminum dispatcher's clipboard pinned to the wall, with a stack of carbon-copy booking slips clamped under a binder clip. The topmost slip is fully visible — origin/destination, MC, agreed rate, margin — and below it you can see the edges of three or four more slips fanned out underneath, each rotated a degree or two so the stack looks like it was hand-collated at the end of a shift. A red "PENDING" rubber stamp sits in the upper-right corner of the active slip, faded and slightly off-axis like it was banged down in a hurry.

Approval is a two-button action — a green "APPROVE" stamp and a red "REJECT" stamp, both styled like ink pads with a subtle drop shadow so they feel pressable. When the top slip is resolved, it slides off the clipboard and the next slip rises into view. The carbon-paper aesthetic — slightly off-white background, monospace typewriter fields, perforated edge along the left margin — sells the metaphor without breaking the Freight Terminal palette around it.

Concept 8 · Ticker Tape

Lens: Physical / Tactile

The screen is a continuous strip of thermal-printer receipt paper feeding down from a printhead at the top of the viewport, like the old freight-office Telex that chattered out tenders all day. Each new booking prints as a torn-edge receipt segment — perforated top and bottom, monospace courier ink, slightly faded at the edges to suggest thermal print — and stacks downward as a single long ribbon. The newest receipt at the top is "still warm" (a faint amber glow at the perforation); older receipts toward the bottom are paler.

Approval happens by stamping each receipt in place — a circular stamp on the right margin, "APPROVED" in green ink with a date timestamp, or "VOID" in red with a slash through the receipt body. Once stamped, the receipt darkens slightly and the stamp stays embossed. Unlike the clipboard concept, this view shows many bookings at once — the broker can scan the whole ribbon, eyeball margins, and stamp in any order. It mirrors how a freight desk actually works during a busy hour: tenders stream in, you triage from the roll, you stamp what you trust.

Concept 9 · Blotter

Lens: Data-Dense / Signal-Rich

A Bloomberg-style trading blotter where every booking is a single dense row and every cell carries a glanceable signal. The broker scans top-to-bottom and reads risk through color saturation, sparkline shape, and in-cell mini-bars — never having to click. The leftmost column is a vertical risk-band stripe (red/amber/green) that lets the eye track outliers as it scrolls. Margin is encoded twice: as a Geist Mono number AND as a horizontal in-cell bar.

Pending bookings live above a subtle divider; approved/rejected fall below in dimmed rows so the worked-through queue stays visible as historical context. A 3-row mini-sparkline per carrier shows their last 3 booked margin %s — pattern recognition for "is this carrier trending cheaper?". A persistent footer ribbon ticks aggregate stats so peripheral vision sees the queue's pulse. Approve/Reject are inline single-key actions, not modals.

Concept 10 · Risk Grid

Lens: Data-Dense / Signal-Rich

A 2D heatmap where every pending booking is a single colored cell positioned by its two most decision-critical axes: Margin % (X) and Case Health Score (Y). The top-right quadrant is the "auto-approve zone" (high margin × high CHS) — cells there glow green and the broker can rubber-stamp. The bottom-left is the "danger zone" (thin margin × low quality) — cells glow red and demand attention. Outliers are visually impossible to miss because they're geometrically isolated.

Cell size encodes booking dollar value (bigger = more revenue at stake), so a tiny green cell is "approve and move on" while a giant red cell screams "look at me first". 60+ bookings fit in one viewport without ever overlapping. Below the grid, a horizontal density strip ("lane heat") shows which lanes are spiking volume right now. Bulk actions live in the top bar: "approve all in green quadrant", "flag all margin<0", giving the broker a queue-clearing power-move that would take 50 individual clicks in a list view.
